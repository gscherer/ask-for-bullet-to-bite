CHAPTER 5


Breakdowns

Seeing Doc again was great. Doc inquired, “How has it been going since I saw you last?” “Good. I am the father of a healthy son.” Grinning with his big brown eyes shining, he asked “Pictures?”


Looking down I said, “I’m ashamed to say it, no pictures.”


“When was your last appointment?” Doc asked while thumbing through the file which was now well into Volume II.


I replied, “I believe it was October 1. I’ve been pretty much pain free since getting discharged. During the last few days, I have started to feel minor pain. It’s the first I’ve had since discharge.”


He inquired, “Does any one thing set it off?”


“No. It’s real shallow and not sustained. I’ve probably taken a total of five or six Talwin tablets (pain medicine). What could be causing it?”


He answered, “We left a piece of the pancreas called the rim in you. It’s possible that scar tissue is pressing against the nerves in that small piece. It could also be neuralgia; created by scar tissue irritating nerves in the bed of the pancreas.”


“What nerves?”


“When Cutter took out your pancreas, he cut nerves that once operated the pancreas. He also took out a portion of the ganglion in the bed of the pancreas. He covered the severed nerves with tissue; hoping to prevent regeneration of the nerves.”


“Why has it decided to get active now?”


Doc said calmly, “It takes about three months for scar tissue to form after abdominal surgery. It has been a little more than three months.” I asked, “Will it get worse?”


“I don’t know Bill.”


I was a little down about my future. Doc just looked at me and said, “Billy you’re alive.” I told him “I have a lot to be thankful for.” I just have to always have that at the front of my memories. Without the skill of my doctors, I wouldn’t be where I am today. 


Two weeks after this appointment, I entered the hospital for eight days of Christmas hospitality. It was another partial small bowel obstruction caused by adhesions.


Diabetes

For several weeks in 1991, I had been eating a lot of ice cream and drinking fruit juice, unconsciously craving sugar. Without insulin, sugars are not absorbed by the cells for energy; blood glucose levels rise. Sugar Doc confirmed my suspicion by measuring my blood sugar. I was to be admitted into the hospital on Monday for schooling to learn about self treatment. Over the weekend, I remained inactive to prevent ketoacidosis, a condition that occurs when there is no insulin to turn food into energy. Instead fat is broken down starting a process that creates ketones. The result of this process is the conversion of body fluids into acids. This is an ICU event.


Physiology and behavior were taught to help diabetics learn to measure and inject insulin, monitor blood glucose levels, eat properly, and exercise, all for maintaining normal blood glucose levels from eighty to one eighty. It was a five day program. Mom and Dad made the trip. We were all concerned about the levels of pain that I was having. After meals the pain would escalate. It didn’t take Doc long to diagnose an ulcer. I asked, “Are you going to scope me?”


Doc answered, “We’ll take a look on Monday.” After the endoscopy, Doc showed me the ulcer on film. By the end of the week, I was feeling a lot better and schooled in treatment of diabetes. It is up to you to treat.


One of my roommates was a big fellow, probably two hundred and fifty pounds on a frame that was easily six foot tall. His dietician worked out his meal plans for the week, which accounted for the doctor’s order for him to lose weight. On Tuesday morning, he was sitting on the edge of the bed when the nurse placed his tray on the night stand. He uncovered the plate and just stared at the two little scoops of scrambled eggs, two small strips of bacon, an English muffin, and skim milk. He looked like a sad puppy dog looking down into an empty food bowl. “I’m gonna get hungry if I can’t eat sweet pies at work and have me a regular breakfast.”


Hospitalization

In the fall of 1993, Talwin failed to control the neuralgia. After six days of not eating much due to nausea, my stamina was severely depleted. Poor nutrition was taking a toll on my body.


I had called Doc to discuss treatment. I said, “Doc, I have been living on a liquid diet and pain pills since I spoke with you last week.”


Doc replied, “I think you need to come in to the hospital to let us determine what’s causing your pain. Do you want to be admitted as an emergency patient or go through general admissions?”


“No. Just go through general admissions.”


Nauseated from pain; and down to one hundred and thirty five pounds from one fifty eight two months before, I checked in for rest from reality. Doc was on the floor, which meant Demerol soon. It was especially quiet even for a Sunday. I was dizzy as I walked up to the nurses’ station with my shades still on.


Doc walked over and held my clammy hand. He said, “You don’t look good Bill. Nurse could you check his sugar? Doesn’t he look pale to you?”


The nurse responded, “It’s not his sugar. He’s at one thirty seven.”


Doc said, “Then he needs a shot. Give him 75/25 Demerol/Phenergan.”


Pain Management Revisited

After a week, I was transferred to the pain school to retrain and practice hypnosis and biofeedback. I would not be given any narcotics for pain. I began therapy by meeting the doctors, who sat around the perimeter of a long table.


“Are there twenty of you now?” 


One of the doctors corrected the count: “Twenty two today.” 


I said to myself, “expensive meeting.” A counselor I met during my previous attendance to the pain school smiled and waved. The group that I worked with during my original attendance had merged with a hospital to treat chronic pain, addiction, and other behavioral diseases.


One of the doctors asked, “Could you tell us your symptoms?”


“I had a really bad pain bout last week. I went to the hospital; and was given Demerol and Phenergan.”


“Do you feel that the use of the techniques being taught here can allow you to reduce and hopefully eliminate your use of narcotic medications?”


I replied, “Since my discharge from the hospital, I haven’t had hardly any pain. The Demerol I took at the hospital was effective in breaking the pain cycle, at least for now. The test will be when I begin to have pain again.” 


Head Doc is one of those guys that hides his baldness by combing his hair from one side over his plainly visible bald head to the other ear. A psychiatrist, that is going to guide me through the rough and jagged edges of intense uninterrupted pain; and he can’t even deal with going bald. After entering his office, he closed the door behind me. He was my psychiatrist during the original training session in 1986.


I rubbed my hands together and said, “Look. I could bullshit you and you bullshit back; but that’s not going to do either one of us any good. I don’t think discontinuing use of narcotic pain medications is possible. There is no way my body can produce sufficient endorphins to relieve this pain.”


He said, “But isn’t that a self fulfilling prophecy? If you tell yourself you can’t do it, you will never achieve the potential good that can be attained by using hypnosis and the other techniques that we teach here. The quality of your life is being damaged by your daily use of narcotics. You’re isolating yourself from your wife, who is considering divorce. Getting off of the narcotics would help your marriage. About ninety one percent of people who have chronic pain get divorced.” As he said that, I slumped down into the chair in despair. 


The stay was progressing normally, until the late afternoon of the fourth day. Pain returned after dinner. Dry heaves started at around three a.m. At seven a.m. I asked a nurse to call a doctor. A gastroenterologist showed up to work through my case with me. He said, “You are such a young man to have to have lived through as much as you have. Are you married?”


“Yes. We’re trying; but it has been really difficult for her. She has had to put up with a lot living with me and the chronic pain. I’m not sure we are going to make it. We are meeting with our marriage counselor and a counselor from your group today. It is very difficult to live with a chronic pain patient. When you say ‘I don’t feel like doing that.’ She believes it is because I don’t love her anymore. I still love her; but I may just be literally too sick to do whatever it is that she wants to do. That can be sex, going out with friends, or just going out together to eat dinner or see a movie.”


As he left the room I asked, “Can you give me anything for pain?” A wave and a smile answered my call as he left the room. I was ready for a shot of anything, any way. Finally a nurse with a shot came into my room. I asked her, “What is the medicine your using?”


She said, “The doctor has ordered Stadol.”


“Stadol makes my heart race. I’ve had it before; and my heart raced.”


She responded by saying, “Let me see if I can get something else.”


I said, “Oh, go ahead and give it to me. I’m so desperate at this point; I’ll take about anything.” 


After the medicine was injected into my body, my heart rate jumped. Eventually my pulse lowered to a comfortable rate. Looking from the bed, my perpendicular view was imploded by spouse and counselors. I was strung out from being up all night mainlining adrenaline produced by my body in reaction to the pain. After the session, I informed them that I would not be attending my exit session with the Doc Troop, spouse, and counselor.


“You’re not going to go to your own exit session?” our marriage counselor asked.


I replied, “No. I am going to call Doc and transfer to a hospital where pain will be relieved by Demerol.” I called Doc and asked to be transferred. “Doc, I’m having acute pain again. I have been up all night with the dry heaves. There are no hard feelings over here. They just don’t treat acute pain. My pain might not be acute in medical terms; but it is very acute for me. This is a bout measured in days, compared to months and years.”


Doc said, “Okay. Well, let’s get you over here and we’ll scope you. Have you eaten anything since midnight last night?”


“No.”


Doc asked, “Can you get a ride over to the hospital?”


“Yea. My wife is going to take me.”


“After you are admitted, go to endoscopy and I will meet you there to help prep you.” 


Admitting is never fun, especially when you are acutely sick. After being admitted, I went to endoscopy. Upon entering, I kept myself busy talking to the nurses. I knew most of them by name. One nurse asked, “What are you doing here?”


“I just missed you so much, I had to come back to see how everyone is doing. And, I like wearing these hospital gowns. They really set the trend in indecent casual wear.” Doc came in and said, “We can do it now.”


It was IV time. The nurse was having a hard time threading the needle into the vein. Doc walked up and said, “Let me try.” I offered a possible explanation for the difficulty in placing the IV. “I think my veins are constricted due to stress.”


As he missed the vein, he said, “I think you’re right.” He asked a nurse to go see if an anesthesiologist is available. He said, “They use these special needles. They won’t let anyone else use them.”


The nurse returned to say, “I couldn’t find either doctor.”


Doc finally got it stuck. “Can I have the medicine before you deaden my throat with that awful spray?”


“Sure. You want the pre-procedure medicine now?” I looked at Doc and said, “Yes please. I have been in pain all day.” Normally the medicine is given to you just before the start of the countdown. The technician moved me into the procedure room. I started to countdown from fifty to zero. Of course you never get near zero: 50,49,48,….At 30 I began going into dreamland. Attempts were made to awaken me. I didn’t respond; so the dose of Valium that is necessary to make sure you don’t wake up during the procedure was given. 


After the procedure, I asked how it went.


Doc said, “You kept us all laughing.”


I replied, “Well, I don’t remember any of it.”


“You had a suture working through the intestinal wall. There was also mild gastritis which shows up on most of your studies.”


“It doesn’t sound like any of this would cause the level of pain that I’m having.”


Doc replied, “There was an area that appeared inflamed like an ulcer. I couldn’t get the scope into position to see it well enough to say for sure. Have you ever taken Talwin by injection?”


“No just the pills.” Doc wrote the orders that included a normal diet.


I was eating everything they brought in, with little pain when Head Doc came in to discuss my treatment. “Hi. How are you today?”


“Good.”


He followed up by asking, “What is Doc planning to do?”


Sitting up in the bed I said, “I don’t know. He saw a suture working into the duodenum and what appeared to be an irritated area that could be an ulcer. It was hidden behind a turn in the intestine. He couldn’t get a good view with the scope.”


“Okay, I’ll contact Doc about treatment.”


Doc came by as I was finishing off the last of my meal. With a quick step he said, “Hello. Hello. How’s it going today?”


“Alright.”


“It looks like you’re eating well. Don’t forget to eat that delicious hospital Jello.”


I said, “I feel pretty good. I haven’t had to take too many pain shots. I don’t know what the night will bring; but so far so good.”


Doc said, “I am planning on letting you go home on Monday if you do well tonight and tomorrow night.”


“Great. Head Doc came by. He said he would call you to discuss treatment.”


Doc nodded and said, “Okay. See you tomorrow.”


You could tell he had more patients to see. My plans for getting discharged were rudely squashed around ten o’clock that night. Talwin injections took care of most of the attack; but keeping my pulse rate from accelerating was hard to do. Thump, thump, thump pounding against my eardrums. Pain signals flew down nerves.


As the nurse gave me a pain shot, she said, “I’m going to call your doctor when my shifts ends at seven and tell him that your pain meds aren’t holding you.” I could hear her talking to Doc on the phone. “I am the nurse taking care of Billy Scherer. I wanted you to know that his pain meds aren’t holding him. It’s obvious that he is in pain. He hasn’t slept any during my shift.” There was a pause. “Okay, thank you.” She came back in to say, “Doc wants you to use Phenergan in combination with the Talwin until he can come see you. He thought it would be in a couple of hours.”


That, I could deal with. After lunch, Doc came in. I could only answer his polite inquiry to how I was doing with, “Oh I’ve been up all night in pain. I’ve been taking shots ever three to four hours. I threw up my lunch.”


With concern he said, “I heard that. The pain was severe?”


“Yea. So other than that, I’m feeling really great.” My words were broken up and washed with tears streaming down my face.


Doc asked calmly, “Do you want a pain shot?” Nodding yes, my hands went to my eyes and then to my robe to loose the tears. “I’m going to give you Demerol. Let me ask a nurse to bring it in.”


I could hear Doc speaking to the nurse “Could you give Billy Scherer a shot of 75/25 Demerol and Phenergan?” Returning to the room he said, “She will bring it as soon as she can. Don’t get too down Bill. I’m going to ask a neurosurgeon to come see you. He takes care of people in pain caused by cancer and other severe illnesses.”


I asked, “How do they treat cases like mine?”


“I don’t know. You can talk to Nerve Cutter, when he comes by.” 


I inquired, “When will that be?”


He answered, “Hopefully tomorrow afternoon.”


Surgery?

Of what? Everything that could be cut is out. With a new hope, I anticipated his visit. We met and went through my history; something that I was getting good at.


He said, “Let me tell you about the procedure. The procedure goes like this. I’ll make two incisions in the middle part of your back. One on each side of your spine. The incisions are about four inches long.”


“The back?”


He responded, “Yes, until we reach the lungs.”


“The lungs?”


He said, “I’ll cut through a rib on each side of your spine. I can then reach the nerves, separate them from the lungs, cut them into, and cap them with a metal boot to prevent regeneration. It will help the abdominal pain, but you may not get much relief from irritated nerves in the pancreas bed. There’s not a lot that can be done about that. The nerves that will be cut transmit messages to and from the pancreas and brain. Since you don’t have a pancreas we can cut those nerves.”


I asked, “What are the side effects?”


Calmly he said, “The surgery can cause you to quickly empty your bowels. Some patients suffer from diarrhea. Since you take a lot of pain medicine, you probably won’t have that problem.”


I said, “I’m always on the other end of that one.”


He added, “When arising, you will need to sit up for twenty or thirty seconds before standing. Otherwise you could lose your balance. It could cause you to fall. I’ll call Doc to schedule the procedure next week. I’ll shoot for Friday. If you decide to have the operation, I want you to be admitted on Thursday to make sure you are ready for surgery.”


That night I talked to Doc about it. He said, “I believe Nerve Cutter can give you relief from a good portion of the abdominal pain.”


With hope I said, “I can live with the back pain. Not this though.”


I was sent home with a narcotic pain medicine patch and a narcotic elixir. Staying heavily drugged, I made it to admission on Thursday. After reaching the floor, Doc came by to tell me “The insurance company won’t approve an overnight stay prior to this operation.”


Bewildered I said, “The insurance company does understand the procedure?”


“Yes” he replied.


Angry at the insurance business I said, “Good grief. They’re going to slice to my lungs and they don’t feel I need to get ready for that?”


He shook his head and said, “I can only tell you what it says here in this note, taken by my office. They won’t approve the extra day prior to the operation.”


Frustrated I said, “Don’t worry about it. I’ll pay to be here. Insurance companies!” My blood wouldn’t clot fast enough; so, I was given a shot of vitamin K. Malnutrition from too many clear liquid diet days was the cause. The insurance covered the cost of the one night stay, since I received treatment that was required before the operation could be performed. 


It was time. The familiar ceiling tiles passed like always as I was rolled to surgery. Call it experience; I could foresee the level of post operative pain. I knew it wasn’t going to be nearly as bad as pancreas surgery. As I was wheeled into the operating room, Nerve Cutter leaned over the edge of the stretcher and said, “I forgot to mention that this procedure can cause impotency.” The pre—op shot kicked in as I stared in disbelief. Afterward, I decided that I would have had the operation regardless of the side effects.


I carried on what I thought was a logical conversation with Doc in recovery. I was sent to intensive care for one night, an extra measure of care given my history and tolerance to narcotics. On November 15, 1993, twelve days after admission, I was discharged knowing what it feels like to be shot in the back with two arrows.


Once at home, I slowly started working to get my legs back in shape and gain strength. After a few days, the pain became increasingly worse and was hardly being controlled at all by the Talwin. I was about blow. I laid down on the bed to use self hypnosis to lower my heart rate and relax my muscles. I started repeating, “I can’t take it anymore…can’t take it anymore…can’t take it ... anymore.” Tears streamed down my face as I said, “Damn it stop.”


My wife did all she could to calm me down. She called Nerve Cutter to get additional pain medicine. She handed me the phone. I told him my symptoms.


He said, “You are having an anxiety attack. Give me a drug store phone number; and I’ll call in an order of Xanex.”


Anxiety attacks to me were something that crazy people had. I had to be at my son’s school for Thanksgiving lunch. I’ll get the pills after the lunch. The turkey and dressing had to be washed down with tea, a trick used when you’re nauseated but need to eat to prevent blood glucose from getting too low. My heart beat got louder and louder. I was weak. I made it to the bathroom wondering which orifice would serve to relieve. Afterward, I walked back to the lunchroom in a dizzy swagger. I was trying to force some food down, when my wife asked, “Billy, do you need to go home?”


I looked at her and said, “I may need to go to the emergency room.” Not wanting to alarm my son, I finished the lunch. Once in the car, she asked if I wanted to try the Xanex before going to the hospital. My hands were shaking. I began to cry uncontrollably. My arms and legs were going numb from hyperventilation. “No. Hospital.”


She wisely said, “Are you sure. Why don’t you try it first.”


Not thinking clearly I said, “No!”


She said, “Alright. That’s where we’re going. Okay? Relax. Do your breathing exercises to calm down.” 


Tears were streaming onto my hands as I grasped my forehead. I entered the emergency room dazed and confused as I said, “I need pain medicine. I can’t handle the level of the pain I’m having.”


In disbelief the nurse said, “Who is your doctor?”


I answered, “Nerve Cutter and Doc.” I yelled, “I want to be admitted so that I can be put to rest.”


A doctor entered and asked why I needed pain medicine.


“I was discharged just the other day for an operation to severe my pancreas nerves. I’m having chronic pain and I can’t take it anymore.”


He asked, “Where is your pain coming from?”


I pulled my shirt away from the bandaged incisions. “It’s coming from this and the pain that caused me to have this operation.”


He looked puzzled and asked, “What did they do when they operated?”


I said, “They severed primary nerves that transmit information to and from the brain and pancreas. It was done to get me out of the hospital and eating without throwing up.”


He shook his head and said, “Rest a minute and let me talk to your doctor.” Another doctor entered to see what was up.


I was still crying, when a nurse told me “We are talking to your doctor on the phone.”


As she walked away, I cried, “Tell him I need to be admitted to get relief from post operative pain.”


She returned with the phone. Nerve Cutter said, “Mr. Scherer you are having an anxiety attack. Did you take the Xanex I prescribed?”


“No. I need to be put to rest; admitted into the hospital for a day or two.”


He answered, “I can’t do that. The hospital administrator would have my neck if I admitted you into the hospital, given that you were discharged just a few days ago.”


I said, “You’re saying that you can’t admit me for relief of pain?”


He firmly stated, “If you will go home and take the Xanex along with your pain medicines, you will get relief and feel better soon.”


In frustration I gave the phone to a nurse. I thought, “Hospitals are supposed to help people in need. I’m in pain.”


A nurse gave me a Xanex. As I walked out of the room to exit, the nurse that I had vented my emotions on said, “He’s approved a Demerol injection. Do you want it?”


“Sure, I would love it.”


My entire energy was directed at ending that pain. The shot hit with swiftness. I began to relax.


A nurse responded to my moan, “What’s that Mr. Scherer?”


I slumped down in the chair of the waiting room; and said, “I think chronic pain is a very misunderstood disease.”


One of the doctors said, “I know. If you go to treatment, you’re looked upon as an addict. At hospitals no one wants to admit on a chronic disorder.”


To me that was a nervous breakdown. Xanex was very beneficial during the next few months which included the guts of divorce.


Many Mini Visits

On one particular day, I began to have pain around my heart. Sugar Doc gave me a physical exam. An EKG showed no trouble with my heart. Sugar Doc said, “Lactic acid builds up in any muscle and causes cramps. You have been experiencing a rapid heart beat for a long time. You may feel pain there in the future. Don’t let it bother you.”


Valium replaced the Xanex. Blood loss from colitis caused Doc to perform a colonoscopy during my next stay from March 29 to April 5, 1994. The acute blood loss from my colon caused me to become anemic. Discharge papers read colitis, depression, diabetes mellitus—insulin dependent, hyperplastic colon polyp.


On July 17, pain initiates around my esophagus. The continuous stress from pain, causes my hand to shake as I grab a spoon. I can’t eat. Tears form slowly. Do you give up and go to the hospital or do you continue to battle? The tears get larger and increase in number as I rub my eyes and wail.


I make the call. “Doc, I can’t take it anymore.”


He asked, “Can’t take what?”


“Pain.”


“Do you need to come to the hospital? I ask you because you’ve had so much experience; you’re the one who needs to decide. Do you need pain management in the hospital?”


I said, “Yes. I’m a mental and physical wreck.”


“Come on in to admissions. I will write orders and send them over.”


After admission, a pain shot put me to rest. By the second day, the call for more pain medicine was extended to five or six hours. I catch myself laughing again, improvement.


The insurance company would only approve two nights in the hospital. Doc tried to get more but their doctor refused. After living with daily pain for months, you’re told to go home after two nights of rest. Your treatment is ended, not by Doc, but by the insurance company. Their doctor suggested that Doc send me home with a narcotic patch instead of hospitalization next time. 


I was grateful for the time stayed. Demerol allowed for sleep of more than four hours at a time; breaking the pain cycle. The more you hurt, the more pain medicine you take. The more medicine you take; the more tolerant you become. This tolerance makes the medicine less effective; causing you to take more pain medicine. This puts you in a viscous cycle.


Stay: 7/17/94 — 7/19/94

Diagnosis: chronic pain syndrome, depression, diabetes mellitus—insulin dependent.


As a condition of my divorce, I tried to obtain ten year term life insurance. The insurance agent asked, “Do you take narcotics?”


I smiled and said, “Sure, every day.”


“You do understand this might prevent you from getting insurance.”


“In my case the drugs are prescribed by my doctor for chronic pain relief.”


In the end, the insurance companies would not bet on my life, not even for ten years.


My divorce was final on December 4, 1994. On December 6, I was admitted into the hospital for relief of intractable pain. Pain that worsened due to the combination of the constant stress of chronic pain and trying to work with little sleep and under the influence of pain medicine.


Diagnosis on 12/9/94: Diabetes mellitus — insulin dependent, esophagitis, gastritis, and neuralgia.


I filed for Disability benefits in May of 1995. On October 25, 1995, I received notice of denial of Disability benefits. On November 6, I cried for relief. Doc had become very frustrated trying to help me. As a gastroenterologist, there wasn’t much he could do except knock me out. I was admitted into the hospital on an emergency basis to get immediate relief by administering narcotics.


Before admission, we met at his office around five. “Doc, I am really worried about the near term and long term effects of living in this pain. I can’t keep doing this. I am living the life of a drug addict. I shoot up in the morning with the purest amphetamine, adrenaline. I come down off the speed with oral narcotics; and take Elavil to sleep. Pain and the body’s response, production of adrenaline, wakes me up in the middle of the night. I take more narcotics to get back asleep. My system is getting pounded everyday with stress hormones. To do this for another ten years is going to take a heavy toll. I’m afraid I’m going to start getting all sorts of stress related diseases after the age of fifty. Is there anyway that we can get the insurance company to pay for more than a couple of nights?”


He shook his head and said, “We can try.”


“Is there anything like frequent flyer miles once you’ve spent over a half million dollars at the hospital?”


“Ah, no.” Rubbing his forehead, he said, “I’m not helping you. I can give you relief with Demerol; but I’m not helping you reduce the pain.”


With face in hands and elbows on knees, I shook my head back and forth saying, “I need help. The other problem that I’m facing is a reconsideration of my Disability claim. They turned me down on the claim I made in May. My insurance company is going to drop me as soon as they can. It’s critical that I get Disability benefits because I can’t work. I stay up all night from the pain. I try to work under the influence of pain medicine. Doc asked, “What reason was given for denying your claim?”


“Oh, they said that my illness is not severe enough to prevent me from working.”


“Did you include depression as contributing to your not being able to work?”


“I filed due to chronic pain and depression.”


“Have you had suicidal thoughts?”


I said, “Doc they come in and go out, when I get really tired and worn down from fighting the pain. I don’t dwell on it.”


“But, you do have suicidal thoughts?”


“Yes. I have a lot of fight in me, Doc.”


“Let’s get you in the hospital and give you 75/25 Demerol/Phenergan through IV push. I will call a new doctor on our staff that has taken a particular interest in pain management. He is a neurologist. His name is Neuro Doc. He will be by to see you.”


Exhaling I said, “Alright. I need the rest.”


Doc asked, “How much weight have you lost?”


“Oh, I’ve lost nine or ten pounds.”


Doc put me at ease when he said, “I am going to admit you as an emergency admit. I’ll call in the orders for pain medication.”


I made my way through admissions on autopilot. When I entered my room, a man was wailing and oozing fluid from his throat. The pump on the breather kept a rhythmic pace; but his trachea tube had become dislodged. As I stepped back from the room’s door, his sister arrived to see him in the midst of confusion.


Looking to the nearest nurse, I said, “I think you need to see about the patient in this room.” 


Nurses moved quickly once they became aware of this bizarre scene.


I had backed away from the room when a nurse leaned over the counter toward me and asked, “Would you like to move to another room?” I answered, “He’s in pretty bad shape. I think you need to put him in a single room. I’m nauseated; so I don’t really want to go back in there.” 


I rubbed my forehead and thought, “just another day at the hospital”. The sound of the breathing machine alone would have been difficult to sleep with. Of course, the hospital is not a place to get sleep. Too many nurses make sure you’re awakened at least once every three or four hours for some type of care or monitoring.


After moving, a nurse walked in and said, “Hello, I’m here to place your IV.”


“I should warn you that I’m a hard stick.”


She stroked my arm looking for a vein. “Well, let’s see what we’ve got to work with here.”


It took a couple of stabs, but it was in. Now if only I could get a shot. It was night when Doc came by. “I see they put your IV in.”


I responded, “Yea. No medicine yet.”


Doc’s head snapped back when he said, “What! Give me a break.”


I said, “Get me one too while you’re at it.”


He spoke firmly to the nurse and said, “Dr. Scherer has not received his pain medicine.”


The nurse replied, “He hasn’t ask for it.”


With a look of disbelief Doc said, “He was admitted for abdominal pain.”


“We’ve been swamped with new admissions.”

In Summary

Since moving to Denver, Colorado from Houston, Texas to live near my son in 2013, I have been taking 12 hour time release morphine sulfate (MS Contin); taking no more than two per day. In addition to morphine, I have also taken no more than one tablet at a time of Hydrocodone-Acetaminophen (NORCO) for breakthrough pain; not to exceed three tablets per day. Using NORCO in combination with morphine sulfate, I have been able to avoid going to the hospital emergency room for an injection of a strong pain medicine. Although an injection of a strong narcotic brings quick relief, it only lasts from four to six hours as compared to twelve hours from the oral extended release morphine sulfate. The hospital is not an option for me now. Every treatment, surgery and procedure available has been performed.


I have HMO Medicare advantage medical insurance. Once I was able to establish my network of doctors, it has served me well. I am very grateful to live in a country that has Disability with medical insurance. Not working with a Ph.D. in civil engineering has been difficult to accept; but it is a fact of life for me. A life that is lived not endured.
